\begin{abstract}
Ecological models can estimate present states more sharply than they justify predictions about recovery, persistence, regime transition, or response to management. A population decline, degraded ecosystem, invasive occurrence, or alternative regime may be well supported while several compatible mechanisms imply different futures. We introduce a finite modelling workflow that separates state estimation from prediction-target reportability. Candidate ecological worlds, observation models, actions, failure structures, costs, and a declared prediction target jointly determine which conclusions are supported. A deterministic report is valid exactly when all worlds compatible with the evidence imply the same target value; otherwise the exact conclusion is set-valued. We characterize the unique coarsest target-safe quotient that removes ecological distinctions irrelevant to the prediction while retaining every distinction required by present and future actions. We then compare adaptive policies by correct deterministic reporting, false resolution, honest ambiguity, and expected cost rather than by full-state identification or generic information gain. In an exact benchmark with 16 latent worlds, a four-level target-irrelevant attribute provides 2 bits of information, exceeding the 0.714 bits supplied by a response experiment of accuracy 0.95. Full-world expected information gain therefore selects the ecologically irrelevant experiment, whereas target-safe design selects the experiment that resolves the declared prediction. The framework is a modular layer for occupancy, population, ecosystem, invasion, regime-shift, mechanistic, Bayesian, and ensemble models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Ecological modelling often answers a state question more confidently than a prediction question. A manager may know that a population is declining but not whether survival improvement, recruitment enhancement, or connectivity restoration will reverse that decline. A lake may be classified as degraded while nutrient reduction and food-web manipulation remain observationally indistinguishable in their expected consequences. An invasive species may be detected without evidence that eradication, containment, or habitat modification will succeed. A system may appear close to a regime shift while competing models disagree about reversibility.

These are not merely problems of imperfect detection. Better detection can resolve the current state while leaving recovery, persistence, transition, or intervention response unresolved. Nor are they solved by selecting the best-fitting model: several models can fit present observations similarly but imply opposite ecological predictions. Generic adaptive design can also miss the point because maximizing entropy reduction may prioritize biologically interesting distinctions that do not alter the declared target.

We formulate the missing layer as target-safe ecological modelling. The user declares candidate ecological worlds, observations, actions, failure factors, costs, and the prediction target. Evidence partitions worlds into compatible classes. The ecological question is not whether one world has the highest score, but whether all worlds in the compatible class imply the same prediction. If they do, deterministic reporting is justified. If they do not, the honest output is the corresponding set of target values.

The contribution is not a new occupancy estimator, identified-set theory, or universal adaptive-control algorithm. It is a modelling framework connecting state estimation, structural uncertainty, ecological prediction, monitoring design, and scientific reporting. Its central distinction is between \emph{state identifiability} and \emph{prediction-target identifiability}.

\section{Relationship to existing approaches}
\subsection{Occupancy and imperfect detection}
Occupancy models estimate occurrence while separating ecological and observation processes. They can provide world probabilities or compatible states to the present framework. The additional question is whether resolving occurrence also resolves the prediction target. Often it does not: presence, degradation, or decline may be established while future persistence or response to intervention remains ambiguous.

\subsection{Partial identification}
Partial-identification methods correctly allow evidence to support sets rather than points. We adopt that discipline. The additional ecological object is an experiment- and target-relative quotient determining which compatible differences matter for future predictions and actions and which can be safely removed.

\subsection{Structural uncertainty and model averaging}
Model selection and averaging rank or combine candidate mechanisms. Target-safe modelling instead asks whether differences among those mechanisms can change the declared prediction. Two biologically different models may be equivalent for one target, while nearly indistinguishable models must remain separate when they predict opposite futures.

\subsection{Adaptive monitoring and experimental design}
Adaptive monitoring and value-of-information methods choose future observations. Generic information objectives reward reduction anywhere in the latent state space. Our objective is narrower and operational: reduce uncertainty in the declared ecological prediction while controlling false resolution and cost. Stopping with an honest set-valued report is permitted.

\section{Modelling framework}
A finite ecological evidence problem contains a latent-world set $W$, experiment family $\mathcal{D}$, observation contract, failure-factor architecture, action grammar, costs, and target map $T:W\to Y$. For design $D$, let $\operatorname{record}_D(w)$ denote the complete record generated under world $w$.

\begin{definition}[Experiment-induced quotient]
For a design $D$,
\[
w\sim_D w' \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \operatorname{record}_D(w)=\operatorname{record}_D(w').
\]
The quotient $W/{\sim_D}$ is the experiment-induced ecological quotient.
\end{definition}

An observed record $x$ induces the compatible class
\[
C_D(x)=\{w\in W:x\text{ is allowed under }w\text{ and }D\}.
\]
The exact prediction report is $T(C_D(x))=\{T(w):w\in C_D(x)\}$.

\begin{theorem}[Honest target reporting]
A deterministic record-based report exists if and only if $T$ is constant on every relevant compatible class. Otherwise the sharp exact report is set-valued.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Unique coarsest target-safe quotient]
For a declared finite evidence problem, there exists a unique coarsest equivalence relation that preserves current observations, preserves target reports under every declared future action, and is stable under action successors. Every other observation-preserving, action-stable quotient supporting the same target conclusions refines it.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[Failure-controlled refinement]
The reliability of a target-relevant class split is bounded by the availability of the observations required to realize that split. Replicates sharing weather, access, observer, sensor, laboratory, spatial, or temporal failure factors cannot exceed the corresponding common-cause ceiling.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Finite risk-limited adaptive design]
For a finite policy family and declared bounds on false resolution, ambiguity, and expected cost, all policies can be evaluated exactly. A least-cost feasible policy exists whenever the feasible set is nonempty; unsupported terminal records retain a set-valued report rather than forcing resolution.
\end{theorem}

\section{Ecological scope}
The same finite structure appears across distinct ecological problems.

\subsection{Degraded ecosystems and restoration}
Observations may establish that a lake, grassland, forest, or reef is degraded while several mechanisms remain compatible. Nutrient limitation, trophic feedback, recruitment failure, or altered disturbance can imply different recovery trajectories.

\subsection{Invasion and control}
Detection of an invader does not identify whether eradication, containment, habitat modification, or no action is most effective. Worlds agreeing on occurrence can differ in spread, persistence, and response to control.

\subsection{Population decline and demographic intervention}
A declining population can be generated by low survival, low recruitment, dispersal limitation, or transient environmental forcing. Trend detection alone does not determine persistence or the effect of supplementation, predator control, habitat restoration, or corridor creation.

\subsection{Regime shifts and reversibility}
Alternative-state models may fit the same current observations while differing in hysteresis and recovery thresholds. Distinguishing current regime membership is not equivalent to identifying reversibility or future transition risk.

\section{Software implementation}
The implementation accepts ordinary finite world and record objects. For example, a system can be within a management threshold or degraded, and degraded worlds can differ in recoverability:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
from ced.experiment_quotient import ExperimentInducedQuotient

worlds = ((0, 0), (0, 1), (1, 0), (1, 1))

def target(w):
    degraded, recoverable = w
    if not degraded:
        return "stable"
    return "recover" if recoverable else "persistent degradation"

state_screen = ExperimentInducedQuotient.from_functions(
    worlds,
    lambda w: "degraded" if w[0] else "within threshold",
    target,
)

print(state_screen.exact_report_for_record("degraded"))
# {'recover', 'persistent degradation'}
\end{lstlisting}

The state screen resolves degradation but not recovery. The software exports compatible worlds, target sets, quotient refinements, failure witnesses, and policy metrics as auditable JSON and CSV outputs.

\section{Comparative benchmark}
\subsection{Latent structure}
The benchmark contains 16 worlds crossing whether a management-relevant condition is absent or present, two possible response types, and a four-level latent attribute that is biologically observable but irrelevant to the declared prediction target. This nuisance dimension is included deliberately: it creates a distinction that is valuable for full-world identification but worthless for target reporting.

\subsection{Compared strategies}
All strategies begin from the same posterior after state screening and apply the same terminal false-resolution contract. \emph{State-only} stops after screening. \emph{Full identification} measures both response type and the nuisance attribute. \emph{Full-world expected information gain} selects the single experiment with the greatest exact mutual information about the 16-world latent state. \emph{Target-safe risk-limited} selects the least-cost experiment with positive probability of producing a contract-valid deterministic target report and otherwise stops with ambiguity.

\subsection{Experiment-choice contrast}
A perfect experiment on the four-level nuisance attribute yields
\[
\log_2 4=2
\]
bits of full-world information. With an equiprobable binary response and response-experiment accuracy $q=0.95$, exact posterior enumeration gives
\[
I(W;X_{\mathrm{response}})=1-H_2(0.95)=0.714
\]
bits. Full-world information gain therefore selects the nuisance experiment, whose target-resolution probability is zero, whereas target-safe design selects the response experiment, whose target-resolution probability is one in the detected-state contrast.

At state-detection sensitivity 0.95, response accuracy 0.99, three independent state observations, and no common failure, target-safe design produces a correct deterministic prediction with probability 0.9944, a wrong deterministic prediction with probability 0.00557, and no ambiguous terminal report. Its expected cost is 4.129. Under the same posterior and reporting rule, full-world information gain spends its follow-up on the nuisance attribute and produces a correct deterministic prediction with probability 0.4500, a wrong deterministic prediction with probability 0.000069, and an ambiguous report with probability 0.5499, at expected cost 2.479. Full identification matches the target-safe reporting probabilities but has higher expected cost, 4.679, because it additionally measures the target-irrelevant attribute.

\subsection{Additional dimensions}
The complete grid varies state-observation sensitivity $\{0.60,0.80,0.95\}$, response accuracy $\{0.85,0.95,0.99\}$, common-failure probability $\{0,0.15,0.35\}$, and shared versus independent failure architectures. Primary metrics are correct deterministic reporting, wrong deterministic reporting, honest ambiguity, expected cost, and effort spent on target-irrelevant distinctions.

\section{Discussion}
The ecological contribution is a distinction between knowing what state a system is in and knowing what future or intervention consequence the evidence supports. Those tasks coincide only when all compatible mechanisms imply the same prediction. When they do not, forcing a point conclusion converts structural uncertainty into unjustified certainty.

The benchmark establishes a second distinction: information about the ecological world is not identical to information about the ecological prediction. A measurement can maximize entropy reduction while leaving the prediction target untouched. Conversely, a lower-information experiment can be scientifically decisive because it separates worlds with opposite target values.

The target-safe quotient therefore changes how model complexity should be judged. Ecological distinctions are retained because they alter present or future reports, not merely because they are observable. Models that differ strongly in internal mechanism can be collapsed when equivalent for the declared target; models that differ only subtly must remain separate when they imply different futures.

Monitoring design should be evaluated by prediction-target resolution rather than sample size or information gain alone. Failure architecture remains important but supporting: replication within one weather window, spatial domain, observer protocol, or laboratory pipeline may add little if all replicates share the same blind spot.

The framework remains conditional on the declared world set, actions, observation models, and targets. It does not make model misspecification disappear. Its value is auditability: every deterministic conclusion can be traced to the compatible worlds it excludes, every retained ambiguity to the target differences it preserves, and every recommended experiment to the class split it is expected to create.

For Ecological Modelling, the central claim is a reusable systems-modelling layer linking ecological state models to defensible prediction and preventing state certainty or full-world information from being mistaken for prediction certainty.

\section{Methods and reproducibility}
The quotient is constructed by grouping worlds with identical records. In the stochastic benchmark, state screening, response typing, and nuisance measurement are separate likelihood kernels. For a belief distribution $p(w)$ and experiment $e$ with outcome $x$, full-world information gain is computed exactly as
\[
I(W;X_e)=H(W)-\sum_x p(x\mid e)H(W\mid x,e).
\]
Every strategy receives the same posterior after screening and uses the same terminal rule: a deterministic target is reported only when its posterior error probability is at most the declared false-resolution limit; otherwise the report remains set-valued. Policy evaluation enumerates latent worlds, screening records, selected experiment outcomes, posterior updates, terminal reports, and accumulated cost. Shared-failure and operational all-negative paths that produce the same record are aggregated before posterior updating. The reproducibility workflow executes the complete test suite on Python 3.10--3.12 and regenerates schema-version-5 JSON, CSV, and LaTeX benchmark artifacts on Python 3.12.
