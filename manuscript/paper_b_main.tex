\begin{abstract}
Ecological models can estimate present states more sharply than they identify the consequences of management intervention. A population decline, degraded ecosystem, invasive occurrence, or alternative regime may be well supported while several compatible mechanisms still imply different responses to restoration, control, supplementation, or disturbance. We introduce a finite modelling workflow that separates state estimation from intervention-response reportability. Candidate ecological worlds, observation models, management actions, failure structures, costs, and a declared target jointly determine which conclusions are supported. A deterministic report is valid exactly when all worlds compatible with the evidence imply the same target response; otherwise the exact conclusion is set-valued. We characterize the unique coarsest target-safe quotient that removes ecological distinctions irrelevant to the management question while retaining every distinction required by present and future interventions. We then compare adaptive policies by correct deterministic reporting, false resolution, honest ambiguity, and expected cost rather than by full-state identification or generic information gain. Exact benchmarks show that state-oriented monitoring can be highly informative yet insufficient for intervention choice, that target-irrelevant model discrimination can waste effort, and that nominally equal replication can have different resolution ceilings when observations share failure factors. The framework is a modular decision layer for occupancy, population, ecosystem, invasion, regime-shift, mechanistic, Bayesian, and ensemble models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Ecological management does not usually require complete knowledge of a system. It requires a defensible answer to a specific intervention question. A manager may know that a population is declining but not whether survival improvement, recruitment enhancement, or connectivity restoration will reverse that decline. A lake may be classified as degraded while nutrient reduction and food-web manipulation remain observationally indistinguishable in their expected consequences. An invasive species may be detected without evidence that eradication, containment, or habitat modification will succeed. A system may appear close to a regime shift while competing models disagree about reversibility.

These are not merely problems of imperfect detection. Better detection can resolve the current state while leaving intervention response unresolved. Nor are they solved by selecting the best-fitting model: several models can fit present observations similarly but imply opposite management actions. Generic adaptive design can also miss the point because maximizing entropy reduction may prioritize biologically interesting distinctions that do not alter the declared decision.

We formulate the missing layer as target-safe ecological modelling. The user declares candidate ecological worlds, observations, management actions, failure factors, costs, and the target report. Evidence partitions worlds into compatible classes. The ecological question is not whether one world has the highest score, but whether all worlds in the compatible class imply the same management conclusion. If they do, deterministic reporting is justified. If they do not, the honest output is the corresponding set of intervention responses.

The contribution is therefore not a new occupancy estimator, identified-set theory, or universal adaptive-control algorithm. It is a modelling framework that connects state estimation, structural uncertainty, intervention prediction, monitoring design, and management reporting. Its central ecological distinction is between \emph{state identifiability} and \emph{intervention-response identifiability}.

\section{Relationship to existing approaches}
\subsection{Occupancy and imperfect detection}
Occupancy models estimate occurrence while separating ecological and observation processes. They can provide world probabilities or compatible states to the present framework. The additional question is whether resolving occurrence also resolves the management target. Often it does not: presence, degradation, or decline may be established while the response to intervention remains ambiguous.

\subsection{Partial identification}
Partial-identification methods correctly allow evidence to support sets rather than points. We adopt that discipline. The additional ecological object is an experiment- and target-relative quotient that determines which compatible differences matter for future management actions and which can be safely removed.

\subsection{Structural uncertainty and model averaging}
Model selection and averaging rank or combine candidate mechanisms. Target-safe modelling instead asks whether differences among those mechanisms can change the declared report. Two biologically different models may be equivalent for one management target, while nearly indistinguishable models must remain separate when they predict opposite intervention responses.

\subsection{Adaptive monitoring and experimental design}
Adaptive monitoring and value-of-information methods choose future observations. Generic information objectives reward reduction anywhere in the latent state space. Our objective is narrower and operational: reduce uncertainty in the management target while controlling false resolution and cost. Stopping with an honest set-valued report is permitted.

\section{Modelling framework}
A finite ecological evidence problem contains a latent-world set $W$, experiment family $\mathcal{D}$, observation contract, failure-factor architecture, action grammar, costs, and target map $T:W\to Y$. For design $D$, let $\operatorname{record}_D(w)$ denote the complete record generated under world $w$.

\begin{definition}[Experiment-induced quotient]
For a design $D$,
\[
w\sim_D w' \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \operatorname{record}_D(w)=\operatorname{record}_D(w').
\]
The quotient $W/{\sim_D}$ is the experiment-induced ecological quotient.
\end{definition}

An observed record $x$ induces the compatible class
\[
C_D(x)=\{w\in W:x\text{ is allowed under }w\text{ and }D\}.
\]
The exact target report is $T(C_D(x))=\{T(w):w\in C_D(x)\}$.

\begin{theorem}[Honest target reporting]
A deterministic record-based report exists if and only if $T$ is constant on every relevant compatible class. Otherwise the sharp exact report is set-valued.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Unique coarsest target-safe quotient]
For a declared finite evidence problem, there exists a unique coarsest equivalence relation that preserves current observations, preserves target reports under every declared future action, and is stable under action successors. Every other observation-preserving, action-stable quotient supporting the same target conclusions refines it.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proposition}[Failure-controlled refinement]
The reliability of a target-relevant class split is bounded by the availability of the observations required to realize that split. Replicates sharing weather, access, observer, sensor, laboratory, spatial, or temporal failure factors cannot exceed the corresponding common-cause ceiling.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}[Finite risk-limited adaptive design]
For a finite policy family and declared bounds on false resolution, ambiguity, and expected cost, all policies can be evaluated exactly. A least-cost feasible policy exists whenever the feasible set is nonempty; unsupported terminal records retain a set-valued report rather than forcing resolution.
\end{theorem}

\section{Ecological scope}
The same finite structure appears across distinct ecological problems.

\subsection{Degraded ecosystems and restoration}
Observations may establish that a lake, grassland, forest, or reef is degraded while several mechanisms remain compatible. Nutrient limitation, trophic feedback, recruitment failure, or altered disturbance can imply different recovery trajectories. The target-safe question is whether the evidence supports a particular restoration response, not merely whether degradation is present.

\subsection{Invasion and control}
Detection of an invader does not identify whether eradication, containment, habitat modification, or no action is most effective. Worlds that agree on occurrence but differ in propagule pressure, source connectivity, or density dependence must remain separate when those differences alter control response.

\subsection{Population decline and demographic intervention}
A declining population can be generated by low survival, low recruitment, dispersal limitation, or transient environmental forcing. Trend detection alone does not determine whether supplementation, predator control, habitat restoration, or corridor creation is justified.

\subsection{Regime shifts and reversibility}
Alternative-state models may fit the same current observations while differing in hysteresis and recovery thresholds. Distinguishing current regime membership is not equivalent to identifying whether the system can be restored by reversing the original driver.

\section{Software implementation}
The implementation accepts ordinary finite world and record objects. For example, a system can be either within a management threshold or degraded, and degraded worlds can differ in whether restoration is expected to succeed:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
from ced.experiment_quotient import ExperimentInducedQuotient

worlds = ((0, 0), (0, 1), (1, 0), (1, 1))

def target(w):
    degraded, recoverable = w
    if not degraded:
        return "no intervention"
    return "restore" if recoverable else "avoid ineffective restoration"

state_screen = ExperimentInducedQuotient.from_functions(
    worlds,
    lambda w: "degraded" if w[0] else "within threshold",
    target,
)

print(state_screen.exact_report_for_record("degraded"))
# {'restore', 'avoid ineffective restoration'}
\end{lstlisting}

The state screen resolves degradation but not restoration response. The software exports compatible worlds, target sets, quotient refinements, failure witnesses, and policy metrics as auditable JSON and CSV outputs.

\section{Comparative benchmark}
\subsection{Generic state--response design}
The benchmark crosses two dimensions: whether a focal ecological condition requiring management is present, and which of two intervention-response types governs the system when that condition is present. This structure represents decline plus recoverability, invasion plus controllability, degradation plus restoration response, or regime state plus reversibility. State observations are imperfect. Response experiments are informative only in the relevant state. Observation paths may share or diversify failure factors.

\subsection{Compared strategies}
We compare four strategies using identical worlds and observation probabilities. \emph{State-only} stops after estimating the current condition. \emph{Full identification} seeks the complete latent world. \emph{Information gain} prioritizes expected reduction of latent uncertainty. \emph{Target-safe risk-limited} selects the least-cost experiment that can resolve the declared response under a false-resolution contract and otherwise retains ambiguity.

\subsection{Questions tested}
The benchmark tests four claims: whether state accuracy can be high while response reportability remains low; whether full identification spends effort on distinctions with identical target consequences; whether information gain selects experiments that are suboptimal for the target; and whether shared failure structures impose resolution ceilings despite increased nominal replication.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics}
Primary metrics are correct deterministic report probability, wrong deterministic report probability, honest ambiguity, expected cost, effort spent on target-irrelevant distinctions, and the probability that additional sampling changes the management recommendation.

\section{Discussion}
The ecological contribution is a distinction between knowing what state a system is in and knowing what will happen if it is managed. Those tasks coincide only when all compatible mechanisms imply the same intervention response. When they do not, forcing a point conclusion converts structural uncertainty into unjustified certainty.

The target-safe quotient also changes how model complexity should be judged. Ecological distinctions are retained not because they are biologically interesting in isolation, but because they alter present or future management reports. Conversely, models that differ strongly in internal mechanism can be collapsed when they are equivalent for the declared target. This gives a decision-relative alternative to both full mechanistic identification and indiscriminate model averaging.

Monitoring design should therefore be evaluated by target resolution rather than sample size or information gain alone. A small manipulation that separates opposite intervention responses may be more valuable than extensive state monitoring. Likewise, replication within one weather window, spatial domain, observer protocol, or laboratory pipeline may add little if all replicates share the same blind spot.

The framework remains conditional on the declared world set, actions, observation models, and targets. It does not make model misspecification disappear. Its value is auditability: every deterministic conclusion can be traced to the compatible worlds it excludes, every retained ambiguity to the target differences it preserves, and every recommended experiment to the class split it is expected to create.

For Ecological Modelling, the central claim is therefore not a new mathematical object in isolation. It is a reusable systems-modelling layer that links ecological state models to intervention prediction and prevents state certainty from being mistaken for management certainty.

\section{Methods and reproducibility}
The quotient is constructed by grouping worlds with identical records. Target-safe minimization uses finite monotone refinement by observations, target reports under declared actions, and successor stability. Policy evaluation enumerates latent worlds and observation paths, classifies terminal reports as correct, wrong, or ambiguous, and accumulates cost. Proofs and implementation details belong in the Supplement; the main text emphasizes ecological interpretation, comparative benchmarks, and model-design consequences.
