# Paper B ecological significance reassessment

## Central ecological problem

Ecological models often estimate the present state more sharply than they identify the consequences of intervention. Detecting decline, degradation, invasion, or an alternative regime is not equivalent to knowing which management action will reverse it.

Paper B should therefore be organized around intervention-response identifiability, not around any one observation technology or focal ecological system.

## General ecological domains

The framework applies when:

- a degraded ecosystem is identified but recovery mechanism remains ambiguous;
- an invader is detected but control response remains uncertain;
- a population decline is established but the limiting demographic process is unresolved;
- a regime is identified but reversibility and hysteresis remain uncertain;
- several structural models fit current observations while implying different future responses.

## Main contribution

The framework determines:

1. whether all worlds compatible with current evidence imply the same management response;
2. which biological or mechanistic distinctions must be retained because they alter intervention consequences;
3. which distinctions can be collapsed because they are irrelevant to the declared target;
4. which observation or experiment can separate the remaining target-relevant classes;
5. when monitoring should stop with an honest set-valued report.

## What is secondary

Imperfect detection, common-cause failure, calibration, and finite-policy enumeration are supporting components. None should dominate the ecological narrative. Their role is to show why apparent evidence may fail to resolve an intervention target.

## Manuscript rule

No private or focal empirical theme should be used as the paper's organizing application. Examples should remain generic or span multiple ecological domains so the contribution reads as a reusable ecological modelling framework.

## Revised headline claim

> Ecological models often identify the current state more sharply than they identify the consequences of intervention. Paper B provides a finite, auditable framework for determining which management-response conclusions are supported, which ecological distinctions remain necessary, and which observations or experiments can resolve them without confusing nominal information with action-relevant evidence.

## Simulation consequences

The central benchmark must vary:

- current-state detectability;
- ambiguity among intervention-response mechanisms;
- alignment or misalignment between latent distinctions and the management target;
- costs of state observation versus discriminating experiments;
- shared versus diversified evidence architecture.

Failure architecture is one benchmark dimension, not the sole result.
